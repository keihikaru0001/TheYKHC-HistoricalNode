## 1. Introduction: What Is a Watch, Really?

A mechanical watch measures the passage of time by counting oscillations of a regulated mechanical system. A quartz watch counts oscillations of a piezoelectric crystal. An atomic clock counts oscillations of a cesium atom.

All of these devices share a single function: **counting the local accumulation of koku steps — τ(x).**

This is not a metaphor. It is physics.

General relativity (and its GKU reformulation) predicts that the rate of proper time τ varies with position in a gravitational field and with velocity:

**τ(x) = τ₀ × √(1 − 2Φ(x)/c² − v(x)²/c²)**

A watch at sea level ticks slower than a watch at 10,000 meters altitude. A watch on a moving train ticks slower than a watch at rest. These effects are small — nanoseconds per day at human scales — but they are real, measured, and used in GPS satellite corrections every second of every day.

Every watch is already a koku density meter. The GANDHARA Watch is the first watch **designed with that knowledge as its conceptual foundation.**

### 3.1 Measurable Differential at Human Scales

The GKU proper time formula gives the following measurable differentials:

**Altitude effect (gravitational):**

Δτ/τ₀ = GM_Earth/(R_Earth × c²) × (h/R_Earth)

For h = 1 meter: Δτ/τ₀ ≈ 1.09 × 10⁻¹⁶ per meter

For h = 8,849 m (Mount Everest summit):
Δτ/τ₀ ≈ 9.65 × 10⁻¹³

**A precision watch carried to the summit of Mount Everest and back accumulates approximately +21 nanoseconds relative to a watch that stayed at sea level.** This is measurable with modern atomic clocks.

**Velocity effect (kinematic):**

For v = 900 km/h (commercial aircraft):
Δτ/τ₀ = −v²/(2c²) ≈ −3.13 × 10⁻¹³ (per second of flight)

The gravitational and kinematic effects partially cancel in aircraft flight — the famous Hafele-Keating experiment (1971) measured exactly this combined effect and confirmed GR predictions to within experimental precision.
