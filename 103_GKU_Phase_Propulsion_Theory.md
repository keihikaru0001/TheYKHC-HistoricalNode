## Abstract

This paper presents the Genki-Koku Universe (GKU) Phase Propulsion Theory — a conceptual framework for propulsion that does not rely on reaction mass or atmospheric medium. The central hypothesis is that a locally induced temporal density gradient field τ(x), generated through a spherical flywheel resonator coupled with an electromagnetic field, may produce a pseudo-propulsive force vector **a** = −k∇τ. If experimentally validated, this mechanism would function identically in atmospheric and space environments, representing a fundamental departure from conventional propulsion paradigms. The theory is grounded in the GKU cosmological model, which treats discrete time steps (刻, koku) as the fundamental unit of spacetime structure. Three experimental hypotheses are proposed, along with a staged verification roadmap from tabletop experiments to CubeSat orbital trials. This work is presented as a conceptual proposal for peer review and institutional collaboration.

**Keywords:** phase propulsion, reaction-free propulsion, temporal density gradient, GKU theory, spacetime propulsion, non-reaction drive, Koku-Universe, TheYKHC

## 1. Introduction

All conventional propulsion systems — from chemical rockets to ion drives — share a fundamental constraint: they require the ejection of mass or the interaction with an external medium (atmosphere, electromagnetic field, solar wind) to generate thrust. Newton's third law demands it. In a closed system, net momentum cannot change.

This constraint defines the boundary of current aerospace capability:
- Atmospheric flight requires air or fuel combustion products as reaction mass
- Space propulsion requires onboard propellant, limiting mission duration and payload
- Deep space travel faces exponential mass penalties from the rocket equation (Tsiolkovsky)

The question this paper poses is: **Is there a propulsion mechanism that operates outside this constraint?**

The GKU Phase Propulsion Theory, developed by Katayama Yoshimitsu (圭光る) under the TheYKHC research framework, proposes that the answer may be yes — not by violating momentum conservation, but by interacting with the spacetime structure itself as an external field.

The theoretical foundation is the Genki-Koku Universe (GKU) model, which treats time not as a continuous background parameter but as a structured field with discrete steps (刻, koku) and measurable local density τ(x). A gradient in this field, ∇τ, may constitute an external field with which matter can exchange momentum — analogous to how a charged particle exchanges momentum with an electromagnetic field without carrying the field onboard.

These four units combine to describe all physical phenomena within the GKU framework.

### 2.2 Koku (刻) as Fundamental Time Quanta

In GKU theory, time is not continuous. It advances in discrete steps — 刻 (koku) — analogous to Planck time but defined as the minimum interval of causal propagation in the local spacetime structure.

The local proper time in GKU is expressed as:

**dτ = dt × √(1 − 2GM/rc² − v²/c²)**

This is the Schwarzschild metric expression for proper time — the same equation used in GPS satellite corrections. In GKU, this equation is reinterpreted not merely as a coordinate transformation but as a physical description of the local "koku density": regions of high gravitational potential or low velocity have faster-ticking koku; regions of low potential or high velocity have slower-ticking koku.

The phase delay between adjacent koku steps is:

**Δφ = τ₀ − τ(x)**

Where τ₀ is the reference proper time rate and τ(x) is the local proper time rate at position x.

### 2.3 Temporal Density Gradient Field

The spatial gradient of the local koku density defines the **temporal density gradient field**:

**∇τ(x) = ∂τ/∂x**

In standard general relativity, this gradient is identical to the gravitational acceleration field (g = −∇Φ, where Φ is the gravitational potential). What GKU adds is the hypothesis that this gradient can be locally induced — not only by mass concentration but by specific configurations of rotating matter and electromagnetic fields.

## 3. Phase Propulsion Hypothesis

### 3.1 The Central Mechanism

The Phase Propulsion hypothesis proposes that a spherical flywheel resonator, when operated at specific rotational frequencies coupled with external electromagnetic excitation, induces a localized and asymmetric τ(x) field around itself.

The induced field takes the form:

**τ(x) = τ₀ + α(Ω, F, Δ) × G(x − xc)**

Where:
- Ω = angular velocity of the flywheel
- F = applied electromagnetic field strength
- Δ = material coupling parameter (function of composition and geometry)
- G(x − xc) = spatial distribution kernel centered on the resonator
- α = coupling coefficient

The resulting propulsive acceleration vector is:

**a = −k∇τ**

Where k is a coupling constant to be determined experimentally.

### 3.2 The Photon Resonance Cavity Mechanism

The physical mechanism proposed for τ(x) field induction involves a spherical (or ring) resonator operating in Whispering Gallery Mode (WGM) — a well-established optical physics phenomenon in which photons circulate around the interior of a spherical or toroidal cavity with extremely low loss.

Key distinction from naive "faster-than-light rotation" proposals:
- The physical matter of the resonator does not rotate at relativistic speeds
- The circulating entity is a **photon field** (electromagnetic mode), not matter
- Photon fields propagate at c by definition — no physical law is violated
- The asymmetric distribution of the circulating photon field creates an asymmetric stress-energy tensor distribution in the local spacetime region

This asymmetric stress-energy distribution is the proposed source of the ∇τ field.

### 3.3 Why This Works in Both Atmosphere and Space

Conventional thrust requires:
```

Ion drive:        Reaction mass (ionized propellant)
Propeller/jet:    Atmospheric medium

Phase propulsion interacts with:
```

The τ(x) field exists everywhere in the universe where spacetime exists. This is the fundamental reason for atmospheric and space universality.

**Detection method:** Precise orbit determination (POD) with GPS + accelerometer cross-validation  
**Falsification criterion:** No trajectory anomaly exceeding 3σ after 12 months of operation

## 5. Propulsion Model and Performance Projections

### 5.1 General Thrust Equation

The generalized thrust model proposed for engineering design:

**T = K₀ × Δᵃ × Ωᵇ × Fᶜ × Π(S, η, H)**

Where:
- K₀ = baseline coupling constant (to be determined experimentally)
- Δ = material coupling parameter
- Ω = angular velocity (rad/s)
- F = electromagnetic field strength (T or V/m)
- Π(S, η, H) = dimensionless product of geometric factor S, efficiency η, and harmonic mode H
- a, b, c = scaling exponents (to be determined experimentally)

### 5.2 Constraints and Limitations

The following physical constraints are explicitly acknowledged:

**Momentum conservation:** In a closed system, net momentum cannot change. Phase Propulsion does not claim to violate this — it proposes interaction with the external τ(x) field (analogous to a charged particle gaining momentum from an external electromagnetic field). The spacetime field itself carries the reaction momentum.

**Energy conservation:** The energy input (electromagnetic excitation + flywheel kinetic energy) must account for the kinetic energy of the vehicle. No over-unity claims are made.

**Relativity compliance:** All proposed mechanisms are consistent with special and general relativity. The τ(x) field is derived directly from the Schwarzschild metric.
